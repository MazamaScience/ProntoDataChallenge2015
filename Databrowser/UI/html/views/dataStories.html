Data Stories

Finding stories in the Pronto data is now as easy as poking at a web page. Here, we tell a few of the stories we've found using graphics from the Pronto Databrowser.

Coasting Commuters

Annual members made up 61% of total usage last year, and a crucial question is how, where and why they are using Pronto bikes. To start answering these questions, let’s look at the Weekly Usage by Hour of Day graph. This graph displays the number of bike rides at every hour of the day (y-axis) for every week of the year (x-axis). The more rides, the darker the shade of purple.

Highest use times for annual users are at precisely 8 am and 5 pm throughout the year. As many hoped, annual users clearly use the cycle share system at peak commuting hours.

Where are these commuters coming from and going to? Let’s look at the morning commute first. Who are the hip cats who jump on a Pronto bike first thing in the morning and where do they work? In the following maps, dots represent stations and are sized by the number of departures (red) or arrivals (blue) at each station.

Our hip cats live on Capitol Hill...

...and work either downtown or South Lake Union. A nice downhill ride!!

Riding downhill is fun, but in a fit, young city like Seattle, we would hope that at least some of these commuters also bike back up the hill at the end of the day. So let’s take a look at station arrivals for the PM commute.

Oh-oh. Only a few people seem to be riding bikes back up the hill after work. In fact, many of the stations with highest arrival count for the evening commute are even closer to the water than those of the morning commute, continuing the Pronto bicycles’ journey downhill. Doesn’t anyone bike uphill?

Let’s see what the trip data say about the elevation of the average Pronto bike over the course of a day.

Holy Crap! Pronto bikes -- on average -- come screaming off of Capitol Hill during the morning commute and continue downhill all the way to the bottom of Elliot Bay by the late eving. (Of course this doesn't actually happen. Through the wonders of "rebalancing", Pronto bikes are transported uphill without riders, ready to coast downhill again. But these riderless trips are not in the data.)

While it’s fabulous that people are using Pronto bikes to commute, it’s a little less fabulous that the system is dependent on shuttling bikes uphill to keep it going. Still, as the old saying goes: "A guy on a bike is worth two in a car!"

Station Analyses

The databrowser is also useful for examining specific stations and seeing patterns of use that are similar and different between stations.

Let’s start by looking at an abbreviated bar graph of total station usage, including annual and short-term users, to see which stations have the highest usage and which stations have the lowest.

Pier 69 is the winner in terms of total station usage, followed closely by several others downtown and in South Lake Union. Taking Pier 69 as an example, let’s do a closer analysis of how and by whom this particular station is used.

(The chart above is for Pier 69 only. It could use better labeling!)

The vast majority of trips from the Pier 69 Station are made by short-term users, presumably tourists. In the density graph below, where a darker purple indicates a higher number of trips, we can see that trips from Pier 69 are highly correlated with season and spread throughout the middle of the day.

In the bar graph below, we can see that the primary destination from Pier 69 happens to also be Pier 69.

Why are so many short term users taking bikes from one station and returning them to the same one? Probably because the waterfront is a lovely bike ride, and Myrtle-Edwards Park extends north from the Pier 69 station, making the natural return point for a casual bike ride Pier 69 yet again. The second most popular destination is the Aquarium, also primarily a tourist attraction.

The third most popular destination, however, is King Street Station and this becomes the most popular station when we subset for annual members and evening commute times.

Why would King Street Station be such a popular destination? Probably as a part of the commute home: downtown workers in a rush might walk down the hill and hop on a Pronto bike to catch their train. (Bikeshare and rail really do fit together well. When will we get Pronto stations at lightrail stations to address the "last mile" problem?)

For contrast, let’s examine a station on Capitol Hill: 12th Ave and East Denny Way. The start station is not a popular end destination in this case and riders are headed either downtown or to Cal Anderson Park.

Also noteworthy: at this station the vast majority of users are annual pass holders.

This station also exhibits a different hourly use pattern. There are some very obvious morning commuters from this Capitol Hill Station but there is no equivalent evening rush. As we showed above, the ride up Capitol Hill is apparently too steep, even for Seattle's fit young people.
